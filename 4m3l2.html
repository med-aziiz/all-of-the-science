Module 3: Branches of Chemistry

Lesson 3.2: Inorganic Chemistry Essentials

Inorganic chemistry deals with compounds that generally do not contain C–H bonds. This lesson covers metals, coordination compounds, and the properties of acids, bases, and salts.

Step 1: What Is Inorganic Chemistry?

Definition: The study of substances that are not primarily based on carbon-hydrogen bonds.

Analogy: If organic chemistry uses flexible carbon Legos, inorganic chemistry uses sturdier building blocks like metals and minerals.

Step 2: Metals and Their Properties

  • Metals are malleable, ductile, and good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Examples: Copper (excellent conductor) and Aluminum (lightweight and corrosion resistant).

Step 3: Coordination Compounds

Definition: Compounds with a central metal ion bonded to ligands.

Example: [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ is a coordination complex used in various applications.

Step 4: Acids, Bases, and Salts

  • Acids: Donate H⁺ (e.g., HCl).
  • Bases: Accept H⁺ or donate OH⁻ (e.g., NaOH).
  • Salts: Formed by neutralization (e.g., NaCl).
