Course 6: Earth Science

Module 1: Geology & Earth's Structure

Lesson 1.1: Earth's Layers & Plate Tectonics

This lesson explores what makes up our planet and how its outer shell moves. We’ll learn about the different layers of the Earth and the theory of plate tectonics that explains how these layers interact.

Step 1: The Layers of the Earth

What Are the Earth's Layers?

  • Crust: The thin, outermost layer where we live.
    Example: The ground beneath your feet.
  • Mantle: The thick layer beneath the crust made of solid rock that slowly flows (like a very thick liquid).
    Analogy: Imagine the mantle as thick honey that moves very slowly.
  • Outer Core: A layer of molten (liquid) metal, mostly iron and nickel.
  • Inner Core: The solid, extremely hot center composed mainly of iron and nickel.

Scientists study these layers by analyzing seismic waves from earthquakes. (Seismic waves change speed and direction depending on the material they travel through.)

Step 2: Plate Tectonics

What Is Plate Tectonics?

Plate tectonics is the theory that the Earth’s outer shell (the lithosphere) is divided into large, rigid plates that move over the softer, flowing asthenosphere (upper mantle).

Parenthetical Explanation: The lithosphere is the hard outer layer, while the asthenosphere is the part of the mantle that flows slowly.

How Do Plates Move? Heat from the Earth’s interior causes convection currents in the mantle, which drive the movement of tectonic plates.

Analogy: Imagine a pot of soup slowly simmering on the stove—the circulating soup is like the mantle, and the floating ingredients represent tectonic plates.

Types of Plate Boundaries:

  • Divergent Boundaries: Plates move apart (e.g., mid-ocean ridges).
  • Convergent Boundaries: Plates move toward each other; one plate may subduct beneath another (e.g., volcanic arcs).
  • Transform Boundaries: Plates slide horizontally past one another (e.g., the San Andreas Fault).
