Module 1: Classical Mechanics

Lesson 1.1: Kinematics & Dynamics

This lesson introduces the language of motion—kinematics—and then explains dynamics, which tells us why objects move.

Step 1: Kinematics – Describing Motion

Key Concepts in Kinematics:

  • Position: The location of an object in space. Analogy: A dot on a map showing your location.
  • Displacement: The change in position from an initial to a final point. Example: Walking 3 m east then 2 m east gives a displacement of 5 m east.
  • Distance: The total path length traveled, regardless of direction. Example: Walking 3 m east then 2 m west gives a distance of 5 m but a displacement of 1 m east.
  • Velocity: The rate of change of displacement with time. v = Δx/Δt Example: Moving 10 m north in 2 s gives an average velocity of 5 m/s north.
  • Acceleration: The rate of change of velocity with time. a = Δv/Δt Example: Increasing velocity from 0 m/s to 10 m/s in 2 s gives 5 m/s².

Detailed Example:

  1. At time t = 0: A car is at x = 0 with v = 0.
  2. After 4 s: The car reaches v = 8 m/s.
  3. Average acceleration: a = (8 m/s - 0 m/s)/4 s = 2 m/s².
  4. Displacement (using x = v_i t + ½at²): x = 0 + ½(2)(4²) = 16 m.

Step 2: Dynamics – Understanding Forces and Motion

Newton’s Laws of Motion:

  • First Law (Inertia): An object remains at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force. Analogy: A hockey puck on ice keeps moving until friction stops it.
  • Second Law (F = m × a): The force on an object equals its mass times acceleration. Example: A 2 kg object accelerating at 3 m/s² requires 6 N of force.
  • Third Law (Action-Reaction): For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. Example: Pushing a wall causes it to push back with equal force.

Detailed Example of Dynamics: Pushing a shopping cart

  • Mass of cart: 10 kg.
  • Desired acceleration: 1 m/s².
  • Required force: F = 10 kg × 1 m/s² = 10 N.
  • If friction exerts 2 N opposing motion, the applied force must be 12 N.
