Course 7: Life Science and Health

Module 1: Biological Foundations

Lesson 1.1: Cell Biology & Genetics

This lesson introduces the fundamental unit of life—the cell—and explains how genetic information is stored and passed on.

Step 1: What Is a Cell?

Definition: A cell is the smallest unit of life that can function on its own; it is like a tiny, self-contained factory.

  • Nucleus: The control center that stores genetic information (DNA).
    Analogy: Like the office of a factory where important instructions are kept.
  • Mitochondria: Organelles that produce energy (ATP), acting like power plants.
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER): A network that makes and transports proteins and lipids.
    Note: Rough ER has ribosomes (protein synthesis); Smooth ER makes fats and detoxifies substances.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Packages and ships proteins and lipids.
    Analogy: Like the shipping department of a factory.

Detailed Example: In a liver cell, the rough ER produces detoxifying enzymes, and the Golgi apparatus packages these for distribution.

Step 2: The Blueprint of Life – DNA and Genes

  • DNA: The molecule that stores genetic information, like an instruction manual or cookbook.
  • Genes: Segments of DNA that encode instructions for making proteins.
  • Chromosomes: Organized structures of DNA that ensure genetic information is accurately passed on.
    Analogy: Like books arranged neatly on a bookshelf.

Step 3: How Cells Divide and Pass on Traits

  • Mitosis: Process for cell division that produces two identical daughter cells.
    Analogy: Like photocopying a document perfectly.
  • Meiosis: Specialized division that produces gametes (sperm and eggs) with half the number of chromosomes, ensuring genetic diversity.
    Analogy: Like mixing ingredients from two recipes to create new variations.
