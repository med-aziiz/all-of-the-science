Course 8: Social Science

Module 1: Theoretical Foundations

Lesson 1.3: Critical Thinking & Data Analysis

Critical thinking and data analysis are skills that allow social scientists to evaluate information, detect biases, and make informed decisions.

Step 1: What Is Critical Thinking?

Definition: Critical thinking is the ability to analyze facts, evaluate arguments, and make reasoned judgments.

Simple Explanation: It means not just accepting information at face value but questioning and examining it carefully.

Analogy: Think of critical thinking as being a detective who looks for clues and asks questions to solve a mystery.

Step 2: Steps in Critical Thinking

  • Identify Assumptions: Recognize what is taken for granted in an argument.
    Example: Questioning whether a study’s conclusion is based on a biased sample.
  • Evaluate Evidence: Look at the data to see if it truly supports the claims.
    Example: Assessing whether a survey accurately represents the entire population.
  • Draw Reasoned Conclusions: Use logic and evidence to form a sound conclusion.
    Analogy: It’s like piecing together a puzzle—each piece (fact) must fit logically with the others.

Step 3: Data Analysis Techniques

  • Descriptive Statistics: Summarize and describe the main features of a data set (e.g., averages, percentages).
    Example: Finding the average age of participants in a study.
  • Inferential Statistics: Make predictions or inferences about a population based on a sample.
    Explanation: Methods like regression analysis or hypothesis testing determine if an observed effect is real or due to chance.
    Analogy: Imagine you are a chef tasting a small sample of soup (a sample of data) and then estimating the overall flavor of the entire pot (the whole population).
