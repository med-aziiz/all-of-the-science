Lesson 3.2: Abstract Algebra & Number Theory

This lesson explores algebraic structures and the properties of integers, providing a clear insight into groups, rings, fields, and basic number theory.

Step 1: Abstract Algebra – Building Mathematical Structures

Understanding Groups

A group is a set G paired with an operation (denoted here by ·) that satisfies:

  • Closure: For every a, b ∈ G, the result a · b is also in G.
  • Associativity: For every a, b, c ∈ G, (a · b) · c = a · (b · c).
  • Identity Element: There exists an element e ∈ G such that for every a ∈ G, e · a = a · e = a.
  • Inverse Element: For every a ∈ G, there exists a-1 ∈ G such that a · a-1 = a-1 · a = e.

Example – The Integers under Addition:

  • Set: &mathbb;Z (all integers)
  • Operation: Addition (+)
  • Closure, associativity, identity (0), and inverses (negative of each number) hold.

Rings and Fields

  • Ring: A set with two operations (addition and multiplication) where addition forms an abelian group and multiplication is associative and distributes over addition. Example: &mathbb;Z forms a ring.
  • Field: A ring in which every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse. Example: The set of real numbers &mathbb;R is a field.

Homomorphisms – Structure-Preserving Maps

A homomorphism is a function between two algebraic structures that preserves the operations. For a group homomorphism φ: G → H, we have: φ(a · b) = φ(a) · φ(b) for all a, b ∈ G.

Step 2: Number Theory – The Study of Integers

Primes and Divisibility

A prime number is an integer greater than 1 that has no divisors other than 1 and itself.

Example: 7 is prime, while 8 (divisible by 1, 2, 4, 8) is not.

Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Every integer greater than 1 can be uniquely expressed as a product of prime numbers (up to the order of the factors). Example: 60 can be factored as 2 × 2 × 3 × 5.

Modular Arithmetic – Mathematics on a Clock

In modular arithmetic numbers "wrap around" after reaching a specified modulus. Example: In modulo 12 arithmetic, 15 mod 12 = 3 because 15 = 12 + 3, so we write: 15 ≡ 3 (mod 12).

Congruence

Two numbers are congruent modulo n if they have the same remainder when divided by n. Example: 17 and 5 are congruent modulo 12 since both yield a remainder of 5 when divided by 12 (17 ≡ 5 (mod 12)).
