Lesson 3.1: Organic Chemistry Basics

Organic chemistry studies carbon-based compounds. In this lesson, we learn what organic chemistry is, why carbon is so versatile, and explore functional groups and structural representations.

Step 1: What Is Organic Chemistry?

Definition: Organic chemistry focuses on carbon-based compounds and their reactions, particularly those containing C–H bonds.

Analogy: Carbon is like the Lego of chemistry, capable of building endless structures.

Example: Methane (CH₄) is the simplest organic molecule.

Step 2: The Importance of Carbon

  • Carbon can form up to four covalent bonds, allowing complex structures.
  • Example: Benzene (C₆H₆) is a cyclic compound with alternating double bonds.
  • Analogy: Carbon blocks can connect in many different ways, creating simple chains or complex networks.

Step 3: Functional Groups

  • Hydroxyl (–OH): Found in alcohols like ethanol; makes molecules polar.
  • Carboxyl (–COOH): Found in acids like acetic acid; can donate a proton.
  • Amino (–NH₂): Found in amines and amino acids; important for protein formation.

Step 4: Structural Representations

  • Lewis Structures: Diagrams showing bonds and lone pairs.
  • Skeletal Structures: Simplified formulas showing the carbon backbone.

Analogy: A skeletal structure is like a subway map showing only the main stops (carbon atoms).

Step 5: Basic Organic Reactions

  • Substitution: One group is replaced by another (e.g., CH₃Cl + OH⁻ → CH₃OH).
  • Addition: Two molecules combine (e.g., H₂ adds to C₂H₄ to form C₂H₆).
  • Elimination: Removal of a small molecule, often forming a double bond.
