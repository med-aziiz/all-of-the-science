Module 1: Theoretical Foundations

Lesson 1.2: Research Methods in Social Science

Research methods are the tools that social scientists use to gather and analyze information about society and human behavior.

Step 1: What Are Research Methods?

Definition: Research methods are systematic approaches for collecting, analyzing, and interpreting data about social phenomena.

Simple Explanation: Think of research methods as the recipes and techniques used to cook up reliable information about how society works.

Analogy: Just like a chef uses different techniques (baking, frying, grilling) to prepare a meal, social scientists use various research methods to study society.

Step 2: Qualitative vs. Quantitative Research

  • Qualitative Research:

    Definition: Focuses on understanding meanings, experiences, and concepts through non-numerical data (e.g., interviews, observations).

    (Example: Conducting in-depth interviews to explore people’s attitudes about a new policy.)

    Analogy: Like reading a personal diary to understand someone’s thoughts and feelings.

  • Quantitative Research:

    Definition: Involves the collection and analysis of numerical data to identify patterns and test theories statistically.

    (Example: Using surveys with rating scales to measure public opinion.)

    Analogy: Like using a calculator to add up test scores—it gives you clear, measurable results.

Step 3: Common Data Collection Techniques

  • Surveys and Questionnaires: Tools for gathering data from large groups by asking standardized questions.
    Example: A national health survey that asks thousands about their exercise habits.
  • Interviews and Focus Groups: Methods involving direct interaction to collect detailed insights.
    Example: Focus groups used to understand consumer behavior in market research.
  • Observations: Watching and recording behavior in natural settings.
    Example: Ethnographic studies where researchers observe daily life in a community.
  • Analogy: Think of these techniques as different ways to gather stories—surveys collect many brief stories, while interviews collect in-depth narratives.

Step 4: Data Analysis in Social Science

  • Qualitative Data Analysis: Identifying themes and patterns in non-numerical data.
    Example: Coding interview transcripts to find common opinions about social issues.
  • Quantitative Data Analysis: Uses statistical methods to test hypotheses and draw conclusions from numerical data.
    Example: Calculating the average income of a population using survey data.

Analogy: Analyzing data is like solving a puzzle—each piece (data point) helps you see the bigger picture of society.
