Course 5: Astronomy

Module 1: Introduction to Astronomy

Lesson 1.2: Celestial Mechanics & the Solar System

This lesson covers the fundamental laws governing the motions of celestial bodies and an overview of our solar system.

Step 1: Newton’s Laws and Universal Gravitation

  • Newton’s Laws:
    • First Law (Inertia): Objects remain at rest or in uniform motion unless acted upon.
    • Second Law (F = ma): Force equals mass times acceleration.
    • Third Law: Every action has an equal and opposite reaction.
  • Universal Gravitation: F = G (m₁ m₂) / r² explains the gravitational attraction between any two masses.
    Example: The gravitational force between the Earth and the Moon.
  • Analogy: Gravity is like a rubber band connecting two objects—the larger the masses, the stronger the pull.

Step 2: Kepler’s Laws of Planetary Motion

  • First Law: Planets orbit in ellipses with the Sun at one focus.
  • Second Law: A line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal time.
    Analogy: The planet speeds up when closer to the Sun and slows when farther away.
  • Third Law: The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis.
    Example: This law helps calculate distances of planets from the Sun.

Step 3: The Structure of the Solar System

  • Inner Planets: Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars are rocky and small.
  • Outer Planets: Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune are gas or ice giants.
  • Analogy: Think of the inner planets as a cozy family home and the outer planets as sprawling, distant mansions.

Step 4: Gravitational Interactions and Orbits

The balance between gravitational pull and the inertia of orbiting bodies maintains stable orbits.

Analogy: Like spinning a ball on a string—gravity (tension) and inertia keep the ball moving in a circle.
