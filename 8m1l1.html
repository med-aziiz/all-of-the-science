Lesson 1.1: Introduction to Social Theories

Social theories are frameworks that help us explain how societies work and why people behave the way they do.

Step 1: What Are Social Theories?

Definition:
Social theories are ideas and explanations that help us understand and predict social behavior and the organization of society.

Step 2: Major Social Theories

  • Structural Functionalism:

    Definition: This theory views society as a complex system with interdependent parts, each serving a function to maintain stability. (Example: Institutions like family, education, and government work together like organs in a body to keep society healthy.)

    Detailed Example: In a community, schools (education) help prepare young people for work, while the legal system (government) ensures rules are followed, all contributing to social order.

  • Conflict Theory:

    Definition: This theory sees society as a battleground of competing groups, where power and resources are unequally distributed. (Example: Social conflicts, such as those between rich and poor, drive changes in society.)

    Detailed Example: The struggle over economic resources can lead to movements that seek to change unequal systems.

  • Symbolic Interactionism:

    Definition: This theory focuses on the everyday interactions between people and the meanings they attach to these interactions. (Example: How personal interactions and communication shape our identities and social norms.)

    Detailed Example: The way a greeting, like a handshake, is interpreted can vary across cultures and influences social relationships.

Step 3: Why Social Theories Matter

Understanding Society:
Social theories help us understand the patterns and problems in society, guiding research and public policy.

Real-World Application:
They inform decisions in education, healthcare, and government policy by highlighting underlying social dynamics.

Analogy:
Just as a map helps you navigate through a complex city, social theories help you understand the complex layout of society.
