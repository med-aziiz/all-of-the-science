This lesson delves into discrete mathematics—the study of distinct, countable objects—and combinatorics, the art of counting and arrangement.

Step 1: The Foundations of Discrete Mathematics

What is Discrete Mathematics?

Discrete mathematics deals with separate and countable objects (like integers, graphs, or logical statements) as opposed to continuous mathematics which studies smooth curves.

Graph Theory – Understanding Networks

Definition: Graph theory studies graphs composed of vertices (nodes) and edges (connections).

  • Vertices: Represent individual points or objects.
  • Edges: Represent connections between vertices.

Logic and Boolean Algebra

Boolean algebra deals with variables that take on two values (true and false) and is the foundation for computer logic.

  • AND: True only if both inputs are true.
  • OR: True if at least one input is true.
  • NOT: Inverts the truth value.

Step 2: Combinatorics – The Art of Counting

Basic Counting Principles

Combinatorics involves counting, arranging, and analyzing discrete structures.

Permutations: Counting Ordered Arrangements

Definition: A permutation is an arrangement of items where order matters.
Example: Arranging 3 books: 3! = 3 × 2 × 1 = 6 ways.

Combinations: Counting Unordered Selections

Definition: A combination is a selection where order does not matter.
Example: Choosing 2 fruits out of 4: &binom{4}{2} = 6 possible selections.

The Pigeonhole Principle

If you have more items than containers, at least one container must hold more than one item.
Example: 13 socks placed into 12 drawers means one drawer holds at least 2 socks.

Advanced Counting Techniques

  • Recursion and Generating Functions: Breaking down complex counting problems into simpler parts using power series.
  • Inclusion-Exclusion Principle: Accurately counting elements in overlapping sets by adding individual counts and subtracting intersections.
