Module 4: Specialized Topics

Lesson 4.1: Exoplanets & Astrobiology

This lesson examines the discovery and study of planets outside our solar system (exoplanets) and the search for extraterrestrial life (astrobiology).

Step 1: Finding Exoplanets

  • Transit Method: Detects dips in a star’s brightness when a planet passes in front.
    Example: NASA’s Kepler mission.
    Analogy: Like a bug passing in front of a streetlight.
  • Radial Velocity Method: Measures the star’s wobble via Doppler shifts.
    Example: Discovery of 51 Pegasi b.
    Analogy: A dancer’s subtle movements revealing a partner.
  • Other Methods: Direct imaging and microlensing also help find exoplanets.

Step 2: Understanding Exoplanets and Their Habitability

  • Variety of Exoplanets: Range from gas giants to rocky, Earth-like planets.
    Example: The TRAPPIST-1 system has several Earth-sized planets.
  • Studying Their Atmospheres: Transmission spectroscopy reveals atmospheric composition.
    Analogy: Tasting a sip of soup to identify its ingredients.
  • The Habitable Zone: Also called the "Goldilocks Zone," where conditions may allow liquid water.

Step 3: The Search for Life – Astrobiology

  • Definition: Astrobiology studies the potential for life in the universe, including the conditions required and possible biosignatures.
  • Example: Studying extremophiles on Earth to understand life in harsh extraterrestrial environments.
  • Analogy: Like a detective gathering clues to solve a mystery.
